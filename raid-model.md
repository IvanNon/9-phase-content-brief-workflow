# The RAID Model

A 4W framework for user intent analysis: WHO, WHAT, WHERE, WHY.

The RAID model goes beyond surface-level intent classification (informational/commercial/transactional/navigational) to ask deeper questions about who is searching, what they actually want, where they are in their journey, and why this query, why now.

---

## Why RAID

Most keyword research stops at intent classification. "This keyword is informational" or "this is commercial intent." That's necessary but insufficient.

The RAID model asks four sharper questions that reveal the user behind the query. Briefs grounded in RAID analysis produce content that resonates because the writer knows who they're writing for and what success looks like for that reader.

---

## The four dimensions

### WHO is searching this keyword?

Surface-level: anyone interested in the topic.

Substantive: a specific kind of person at a specific moment in their life. RAID asks you to commit to specifics, even when the answer is "multiple personas at different life stages."

**Sub-questions:**

- **Demographics.** Age range. Professional context (if relevant). Life stage (student, early career, parent, retiree, etc.). Income context if relevant to query.
- **Knowledge level.** Beginner who has never encountered the topic. Intermediate who knows the basics. Expert who's diving deep on a specific aspect.
- **Emotional state.** Curious (low stakes). Frustrated (something isn't working). Urgent (need to act soon). Exploratory (browsing options).
- **Decision-making stage.** Just learning (no decision pending). Comparing options (decision pending but undecided). Ready to act (decision made, executing).

**Example for the query "how to file a tax extension":**

- Demographics: working adult, US tax-paying, age 25-65, often someone with self-employment income or unexpected complications
- Knowledge level: intermediate (knows what a tax extension is, doesn't know the mechanics)
- Emotional state: stressed-to-anxious (deadline pressure, possible penalty fear)
- Decision stage: ready to act (already decided to file extension, executing)

---

### WHAT are they actually trying to accomplish?

Surface-level: the literal words they typed.

Substantive: the underlying need that produced those words.

**Sub-questions:**

- **Surface query.** What did they actually type into the search box?
- **Underlying need.** What is the real goal beneath the literal query? Often this is "answer the surface question PLUS related questions they didn't ask but will need answers to."
- **Implicit assumptions.** What do they assume to be true that the content needs to confirm or correct?
- **Success state.** What does it look like for them after engaging with the content? What did they want to walk away with?

**Example for the query "how to file a tax extension":**

- Surface query: literally just "how to file a tax extension"
- Underlying need: complete an extension filing successfully without penalty, plus understand what comes next (when extension expires, what they still need to file)
- Implicit assumptions: that extensions are "extra time to file" (they are, but NOT extra time to pay — common misconception)
- Success state: extension filed successfully, understanding of what they still owe and when, confidence they won't face surprise penalties

---

### WHERE are they in their journey?

Surface-level: top/middle/bottom of funnel.

Substantive: a specific stage with specific predecessor and successor questions.

**Sub-questions:**

- **Primary stage.** Top of funnel (problem recognition: "do I have a problem"), middle of funnel (solution exploration: "what are my options"), bottom of funnel (decision/action: "let me execute"), post-action (validation/troubleshooting: "did I do this right").
- **Secondary stages also served.** Even if the primary stage is bottom-of-funnel, content might also serve middle-of-funnel readers if structured well.
- **Predecessor questions.** What did the searcher search BEFORE this query? Understanding this helps frame the introduction (don't repeat what they already know).
- **Successor questions.** What will the searcher search AFTER this query? Understanding this helps the content anticipate next steps.

**Example for the query "how to file a tax extension":**

- Primary stage: bottom-of-funnel (executing the decision to file extension)
- Secondary stage served: post-action validation (people checking that they're doing it right after starting)
- Predecessor questions: "what is a tax extension," "do I qualify for tax extension," "tax extension penalty"
- Successor questions: "tax extension deadline 2026," "how to estimate taxes owed," "what if I can't pay my taxes"

---

### WHY this query, why now?

Surface-level: they need information.

Substantive: a specific triggering event motivated the search at this specific moment.

**Sub-questions:**

- **Triggering event.** What happened in the searcher's life that produced this query right now? A life change, news event, conversation, problem appearance, deadline approaching.
- **Motivation.** Curiosity (low intensity, exploration). Urgency (action required, time-sensitive). Obligation (someone or something requires this). Opportunity (potential gain identified).
- **Stakes.** Low (information is nice to have). Medium (matters but not critical). High (significant consequences if wrong action taken or no action taken).

**Example for the query "how to file a tax extension":**

- Triggering event: tax deadline approaching, person realizes they cannot file on time (missing documents, complexity, illness, life event)
- Motivation: urgent + obligation (deadline + legal/financial consequence of missing it)
- Stakes: high (potential late-filing penalties, interest charges, possible audit exposure if wrong)

## How to apply RAID in Phase 2

Phase 2 of the workflow asks for a complete RAID analysis. Here's how to apply it without spending excessive time:

### Step 1: Read the SERP

Before answering RAID questions, look at the top 5-10 ranking results for the primary keyword. The SERP itself reveals a lot:
- What angles are being taken (suggests what searchers respond to)
- What experience level the content targets (beginner-to-expert)
- What tone is dominant (clinical, conversational, urgent, reassuring)
- What additional questions appear in "People Also Ask" (hints at successor/predecessor questions)

### Step 2: Read 2-3 forum/Reddit threads about the topic

Forums and Reddit threads expose the actual user voice. Searchers often post questions that reveal:
- Their actual emotional state
- Their actual knowledge gaps
- Their actual life context
- The triggering events that led to their question

15 minutes of forum reading often reveals more about user intent than an hour of analyzing search data.

### Step 3: Answer the RAID questions explicitly

Don't skip to general answers. Force yourself to answer each sub-question specifically:
- WHO: demographics, knowledge level, emotional state, decision stage
- WHAT: surface query, underlying need, implicit assumptions, success state
- WHERE: primary stage, secondary stages, predecessor questions, successor questions
- WHY: triggering event, motivation, stakes

### Step 4: Translate RAID into intent distribution

After answering RAID, translate into intent percentages that sum to 100%:
- What percentage of searchers are informational (just learning)?
- What percentage are commercial (researching to make a decision)?
- What percentage are transactional (ready to execute)?
- What percentage are navigational (looking for specific source)?

The RAID analysis grounds these percentages. If RAID revealed that 80% of searchers are at "ready to act," then transactional intent is dominant.

## How RAID feeds the rest of the workflow

RAID isn't just an exercise. Its outputs feed downstream phases:

- **Phase 3 (Keyword strategy):** RAID reveals which sub-topics matter most to the dominant user group. Keyword cluster priorities follow.

- **Phase 4 (Sub-queries):** Predecessor and successor questions from RAID become candidate sub-queries.

- **Phase 5 (Content architecture):** Pain points from RAID become section topics. Knowledge level from RAID determines depth/explanation level. Emotional state determines tone.

- **Phase 9 (Copywriter directive):** RAID's WHO and WHY produce the audience profile and tone guidance the writer needs.

A strong RAID analysis in Phase 2 makes every subsequent phase faster and more grounded. A weak RAID analysis (generic answers) makes every subsequent phase weaker.

---

## RAID common pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Generic answers

"WHO: anyone interested in this topic." That's not RAID; that's evading the question. Force specificity even if it feels uncomfortable to commit to one persona. You can name multiple personas if needed, but each one must be specific.

### Pitfall 2: Skipping the WHY dimension

WHY is the hardest dimension because it requires imagining what triggered the search at this specific moment. Many practitioners skip it or give a generic "they want information." But WHY is often the most actionable dimension — it tells you what the introduction needs to do (acknowledge the trigger, calibrate to stakes).

### Pitfall 3: Answering RAID without reading the SERP or forums

RAID is more accurate when grounded in actual user voice. Skipping the SERP/forum read produces RAID answers that feel right but aren't.

### Pitfall 4: Treating RAID as a checklist instead of insight generation

The point of RAID isn't to fill in fields. It's to produce *insight* about the user that changes how the brief is written. If your RAID output is just a list of attributes that doesn't shift downstream phases, you treated it as a checklist.

## Sample RAID output

**Query:** "best running shoes for plantar fasciitis"

```markdown
### WHO
- Demographics: adult runners, age 30-65, often newer to running OR experienced runners dealing with overuse injury
- Knowledge level: intermediate-to-advanced (knows what plantar fasciitis is, knows shoes matter, doesn't know specific recommendations)
- Emotional state: frustrated (current shoes aren't working) + hopeful (wants solution) + cautious (worried about wasting money)
- Decision stage: comparing options — knows they need different shoes, evaluating which

### WHAT
- Surface query: "best running shoes for plantar fasciitis"
- Underlying need: identify 3-5 specific shoes that have proven helpful for plantar fasciitis sufferers, understand what features to look for, understand price/value tradeoffs
- Implicit assumptions: that there ARE shoes specifically suited for this condition (true), that "best" can be determined without seeing their specific foot/gait (partially false — individual fit matters)
- Success state: confidence in 1-2 specific shoe recommendations to try, understanding of what features to prioritize, sense of price range to budget for

### WHERE
- Primary stage: middle-of-funnel (comparing options before purchase)
- Secondary stages served: top-of-funnel (some readers still confirming plantar fasciitis is causing their pain), bottom-of-funnel (some ready to buy if convinced)
- Predecessor questions: "plantar fasciitis symptoms," "plantar fasciitis treatment," "what shoes for foot pain"
- Successor questions: "where to buy [specific shoe]," "[specific shoe] reviews," "plantar fasciitis insoles"

### WHY
- Triggering event: persistent or worsening foot pain affecting their running routine; possibly diagnosis from doctor or self-diagnosis from symptom matching
- Motivation: urgent (want pain to stop) + opportunity (want to keep running, hoping shoes can solve it)
- Stakes: medium-to-high (chronic foot pain affects life quality; running is identity for many)

### Intent distribution
- Informational: 30% (understanding what makes a shoe good for this condition)
- Commercial: 60% (comparing specific shoe options)
- Transactional: 10% (ready to click "buy")

### User journey stages
1. Pain acknowledgment: "I have foot pain when I run" (predecessor)
2. Diagnosis: "this might be plantar fasciitis" (predecessor)
3. Solution research: "what shoes help" ← THIS QUERY
4. Specific evaluation: "[shoe brand] for plantar fasciitis" (successor)
5. Purchase: "buy [shoe]" (successor)

### Pain points (gaps in current SERP)
1. Most articles list 10-20 shoes without clear ranking — paralysis of choice
2. Most articles don't differentiate between high-arch vs flat-foot needs (both can have plantar fasciitis but need different shoes)
3. Few articles connect shoe recommendations to runner type (treadmill vs trail vs road)
4. Most don't address the "broken in vs new" question — current sufferers may want shoes they can wear immediately, not break in over weeks
```

This level of RAID specificity gives the brief writer a real audience to write for, not a generic "people who might search this."
